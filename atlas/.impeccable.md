## Design Context

### Users

Atlas is used by fleet and operations teams managing several aircraft from a local ground station. Operators need to assess fleet readiness, inspect an individual drone, validate camera and gimbal operation before deployment, execute missions, and understand live or historical vehicle state. The interface must support rapid switching between aircraft without losing target identity or operational context.

### Brand Personality

Exploratory, capable, and operational. Atlas should feel feature-rich and technically powerful while remaining trustworthy around live vehicle control. The product should encourage investigation and system validation without making consequential actions feel casual.

### Aesthetic Direction

Use an information-dense, industrial operations-console direction with clear hierarchy and progressive disclosure. Prefer a refined light workspace with purposeful status colour, strong aircraft identity, and distinct boundaries between observation, manual control, and mission execution. Avoid generic dashboard card grids, decorative sci-fi styling, and visual effects that compete with live operational state.

### Design Principles

1. Keep aircraft identity and connection state persistent whenever an operator can issue a command.
2. Separate observation, manual payload control, and autonomous mission execution while allowing operators to move deliberately between them.
3. Make pre-mission validation a first-class workflow: live video, perception health, and gimbal checks belong on the drone operations surface.
4. Use progressive disclosure for fleet-scale complexity: show fleet exceptions first, then detailed telemetry and controls for the selected aircraft.
5. Preserve operational history by archiving or decommissioning drones instead of treating removal as an ordinary destructive delete.
6. Communicate command eligibility, acknowledgement, execution, failure, and stale connectivity explicitly; never imply success from a button press alone.
